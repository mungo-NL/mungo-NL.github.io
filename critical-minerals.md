# A New Era for The Rock: Critical Minerals

Newfoundland and Labrador, or "The Rock," is known for its beautiful landscapes, with striking cliffs and rocky beaches. Beneath your feet, the rocks contain important elements for the future of Canada: critical minerals. These aren't just any minerals: they're the building blocks for our high-tech world. From the cell phone in your bag to the electric car you might one day drive, critical minerals are required!

## What are Critical Minerals?

A critical mineral is a mineral that's essential to modern technology. Canada's government has a list of over 30 minerals considered "critical", and many of them are in Newfoundland and Labrador. For example, antimony, cobalt, and nickel are crucial for making the batteries that store energy from wind turbines and solar panels. These minerals are so important that countries around the world are trying to secure their own supplies. The demand for them is increasing as new technologies, like touch screens, require critical minerals.

## Why are they so important for Newfoundland and Labrador?

Our province could be a leader as we have a lot of these minerals, including one of the world's largest nickel deposits at Voisey's Bay in Labrador, and undeveloped rare earth elements deposits in Labrador. On the Burin Peninsula, we have one of the largest fluorspar deposits in North America. The recent lithium discoveries in the province have generated widespread excitement as it demonstrates that "The Rock" still holds many more hidden treasures!

Mining these critical minerals can create new jobs, boost our economy, and help Canada have a reliable supply to create new technology. It also helps us move towards a more sustainable economy by providing the materials needed for green technology. For example, our high-quality, low-impurity iron ore in Labrador is in high demand for making "green steel," a key part of creating a low-carbon future.

## Finding the Treasure: How Do We Locate Critical Minerals?

Finding critical minerals is a bit like being a detective. It starts with looking for clues in the geology of the province. Geoscientists use a variety of tools to explore for minerals. Starting by studying geological maps to identify areas with the right kind of rock formations. Then, using geophysical surveys with special equipment searching for magnetic fields or radiation, which can indicate the presence of certain minerals or seismic exploration for critical minerals, like graphite. The exploration team collects rock and soil samples from the field to analyse with chemistry lab tests. All this information is put together to understand what lies beneath the surface, helping them pinpoint the best places to drill for samples and hopefully, critical minerals!

## Careers in a Critical Minerals Future

The critical minerals industry offers a wide range of career opportunities, from hands-on work in the field to technical jobs in a lab or office. Currently, there is a hiring crisis with not enough new talent entering the mining industry. These careers are more than just mining; it's a whole chain of events, from finding the mineral to processing it and turning it into a final product.

- **Geoscientists** use their knowledge of rocks and Earth's history to find new mineral deposits.
- **Mining engineers** design and manage extraction of minerals from the ground.
- **Heavy equipment operators and technicians** are essential for running the machinery used in exploration and mining.
- **Data scientists** use computers to analyze geophysical data.
- **Environmental specialists** ensure that operations are as sustainable as possible.

These jobs are crucial for an innovative economy in Newfoundland and Labrador.
